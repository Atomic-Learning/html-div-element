In HTML, a <div> element is a generic container for grouping and styling content. It is commonly used to structure and organize other HTML elements. A <div> element is block-level by default, so it typically starts on a new line in normal page flow.

A <div> does not add meaning on its own. It simply groups related pieces of content so your page is easier to read, manage, and build over time.

Why use a div?

Even in simple HTML, grouping content is useful because it helps you:

  • Keep related content together in the page layout
  • Make your page structure easier to understand
  • Prepare your markup for future updates

Basic example

In this example, each <div> groups one logical part of the page:

<div>
	<h1>Student Profile</h1>
</div>

<div>
	<p>Name: Alex</p>
	<p>Course: Engineering</p>
</div>

<div>
	<p>Contact: alex@example.com</p>
</div>

Each section is still made of normal HTML elements. The <div> just keeps each section grouped.

Nesting div elements

You can place one <div> inside another. This is called nesting, and it helps represent sections and subsections.

<div>
	<h2>News</h2>

	<div>
		<h3>Campus Update</h3>
		<p>Library hours have changed.</p>
	</div>
</div>
